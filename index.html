02 — Where did it burn?

The Geography of Destruction

The fires swept across nearly every state and territory. The Northern Territory had the largest total area burned — 6.8 million hectares — but NT fires largely burned remote scrubland with minimal human impact. New South Wales told a different story: 5.2 million hectares burned, 25 lives lost, and 2,176 homes destroyed — the greatest human toll of any state. The two charts reveal this contrast: largest area burned ≠ greatest human impact.

Chart 1 · Geographic Bubble Map — Burnt Area by State
Geographic map with Australia outline. Circle size and colour encode total hectares burned. Larger and darker = more land destroyed. Hover for exact values. Source: Wikipedia / AFAC 2020.

NT had the largest burned area, while NSW had the highest human impact.

Chart 2 · Horizontal Bar — Burnt Area Ranked by State
Bar chart. X-position (quantitative channel) encodes area. States sorted descending. Colour reinforces magnitude using a single-hue orange ramp — no double-encoding with a separate legend. Source: Wikipedia / AFAC 2020.

NT burned the most land (36%), but NSW suffered the highest human toll: 25 deaths and 2,176 homes lost.

03 — When did it peak?

A Season That Wouldn't Stop

The 2019–20 fire season began dangerously early in September 2019 and lasted until March 2020. December and January saw the most intense activity. Daily temperature data from Melbourne Airport (BOM Station 086282) shows the season's relentless heat — and how few days offered any relief.

Chart 3 · Strip Plot — Daily Max Temperature with Fire Danger Category ✦ Custom
Each vertical strip = one day (Sep 2019–Mar 2020). Y-position encodes max temperature (°C). Colour encodes BOM fire danger category (Low → Extreme). This custom strip plot reveals the daily rhythm of heat — days above 38°C (Extreme) cluster in December–January. Source: BOM IDCJAC0010, Station 086282.

8 Extreme danger days (≥38°C) all occurred in December 2019 – January 2020. December had only 7.8mm of rain — a critical drought signal.

Chart 4 · Line Chart — Monthly Average Max Temperature
Line + point marks. Y-position (quantitative) encodes monthly average max temperature (°C). X-position encodes time (ordinal). The single orange hue avoids redundant colour encoding. Source: BOM IDCJAC0010.

Temperature peaked in January 2020 at an average of 27.8°C.

Chart 5 · Bar Chart — Monthly Total Rainfall
Bar chart. Y-position encodes total rainfall (mm) per month. Blue hue chosen to contrast with the orange temperature charts — different colour encodes different variable type. December 2019: only 7.8mm. Source: BOM IDCJAC0009.

December 2019 received just 7.8mm of rain — critically below average.

04 — Why did it happen?

Heat, Drought, and the Perfect Storm

Two climate conditions made the 2019–20 season catastrophic: record-breaking heat and severely below-average rainfall. The scatter plot below — built from 213 days of real BOM data — reveals a clear negative correlation: hotter days consistently brought less rain. The regression line and danger-category colouring make this relationship immediately visible.

Chart 6 · Scatter Plot + Regression — Temperature vs. Rainfall ✦ Custom
Each point = one day (n=213). X-position: max temperature (°C). Y-position: rainfall (mm). Colour encodes BOM fire danger category — same scheme as Chart 3 for consistency. Dashed regression line shows the negative trend. Source: BOM IDCJAC0010 + IDCJAC0009, Station 086282.

Hotter days produced less rainfall — a self-reinforcing drought cycle that extended the fire season.

Chart 7 · Dual-Axis Layered Chart — Temperature & Rainfall Together ✦ Custom
Custom layered chart combining two marks on independent Y-axes: orange area = monthly avg max temperature (left axis); blue bars = monthly total rainfall (right axis). The parallel Dec 2019 spike (high heat + near-zero rain) is immediately striking. Source: BOM IDCJAC0010 + IDCJAC0009.

December 2019: highest temperature of the season + lowest rainfall of the season. This dual extreme triggered the worst fire conditions.

05 — What was the impact?

A Nation in Smoke and Ash

Beyond the statistics lies a human and ecological catastrophe. Scientists estimated that three billion animals were killed or displaced. Smoke blanketed Australia's cities for months. The economic cost exceeded $100 billion AUD — making it the costliest natural disaster in Australian history.

06 — Key takeaways

What the Data Tells Us

The Black Summer was not a random disaster. The data reveals measurable, interconnected conditions — extreme heat, drought, early season onset — that combined to produce an unprecedented catastrophe.

🌡️

December 2019 recorded only 7.8mm of rainfall while averaging 26.4°C — the critical trigger for the season's worst fires.

🗺️

NT recorded the largest burned area, while NSW recorded the highest deaths and homes lost.

📅

The season began in September 2019, two months earlier than historical averages, giving fires longer to build before summer peaked.

💔

33 direct deaths, 3 billion animals displaced, and $100 billion in economic damage — Australia's costliest natural disaster on record.
